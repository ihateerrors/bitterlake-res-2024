The Bitter Lake Reservoir site is a 13-acre property that contains a 21-million-gallon reservoir, a playground, a P-Patch community garden, and about 4 acres of open space. Seattle Public Utilities (SPU) is replacing the existing reservoir with a new, partially buried reservoir that will protect drinking water and preserve water service for years to come. This will open up more space for Seattle Parks and Recreation (SPR) and the Office of Arts and Culture (ARTS) to expand and add to the existing park with new amenities and public art for the community to enjoy.

SPU, SPR, and ARTS are working together to make the Bitter Lake Reservoir site a favorite destination in the neighborhood! The new site design will feature:

  • A new reservoir that improves water security, stormwater systems, and community gathering spaces
  • More open public space, with new park features for the community to enjoy
  • Permanent public art that represents and enriches the community

Thank you to everyone from the Bitter Lake community who has engaged with us and provided feedback on what improvements you want to see from SPU, SPR, and ARTS! Through in-person events and surveys in 2023, we heard your priorities loud and clear:

  • Improve personal safety and security
    • Increasing community activity in the space
    • Improve visibility by adding lighting and trimming overgrown vegetation
    • Increase safety measures
    • Discourage illicit activities
    • Reduce litter
  • Encourage activities that enrich the community
    • Provide opportunities for community activities with a larger gathering area
    • Build accessible walking paths and seating throughout park
    • Expand the P-Patch and playground
    • Provide more trees and natural areas

For more information, read detailed summaries of SPR’s fall 2023 engagement and SPU’s 2023 engagement.

SPU is designing and constructing the new reservoir first because the construction process will change the layout of the land. Once the new layout is complete, SPR will construct the park in the new and existing open space. ARTS will install permanent public art during the final stages of construction, and may also find ways to enrich the site during construction with temporary art. All three departments are collaborating closely with each other throughout engagement, design, and construction.

See the latest reservoir design

SPU recently reached the mid-design milestone, also known as 60% Design Completion, for the upgraded reservoir.

Here’s what’s new:

  • Accessible walking paths around the reservoir: This creates a new family-friendly activity and improves visibility by creating a 360-degree, elevated view of the park.
  • An elevated viewpoint of the entire site: The new walking paths will lead to a gathering space that activates the area around the reservoir and provides an elevated view of the park.
  • Sustainable, on-site stormwater treatment: Four areas around the reservoir will collect stormwater for on-site treatment. These bioretention areas are shaped like drops of water to celebrate the sustainability of these new features. (note to incorporate: There is also the 2 other areas not part of the whirl)

Public Arts

Are you new to the reservoir project? Catch up on what we’ve been doing.

ARTS section

Through informal conversations, hands-on workshops, and public art surveys, people have expressed a desire for public artwork(s) to:

  • Provide opportunities for more engagement and activity at the Bitter Lake Reservoir
  • Celebrate the diversity of the neighborhood
  • Tell the history of the neighborhood
  • Communicate the relationship to water at the site

Direction for how and where public art serves the community at the Bitter Lake Reservoir is still forming. Stop by the ARTS table to see and play with a to-scale model of the proposed Bitter Lake Reservoir site and brainstorm ideas for public art projects.

Have any questions or comments about public art direction for Bitter Lake Reservoir Improvement Project? Contact: kate.clark-c@seattle.gov

Since August 2023, planning artist Kate Clark has met with various Bitter Lake community groups to learn about desires about direction for public art for the Bitter Lake Reservoir Improvement Project. Meetings with locals have included residents of the Interurban SHAG, Bitter Lake P-Patch Gardeners, residents of North Star Permanent Housing, Open House attendees, and members of the Eritrean community.

After more community conversation and direction, Kate Clark, in collaboration with the Seattle Office of Arts and Culture (ARTS), SPU and SPR, will work to narrow a shortlist of potential projects for further community input on preference in Winter 2024. Finally, ARTS will draft public calls for regional and national artists to apply to these opportunities in 2024.

What's next

SPU, SPR, and ARTS will continue working together to design this new community destination. Here’s what we’ll be working on next:

  • SPU will continue working on the reservoir design and keep engaging with the community, helping educate residents about the reservoir’s role in Seattle’s water system.
  • SPR will select a preferred design, further refine the park design, and continue collecting public feedback.
  • ARTS will continue engaging with the community to identify public art concepts and start the process of commissioning artist(s) to create public artwork(s).
